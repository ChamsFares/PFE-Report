\chapter{Foundations and Protocols}
\section{Introduction}
This chapter provides an explanation of the development of data center network architecture based on the demands of the digital economy. The theory of network layering and planes is covered to set a basis for protocols used in this project. The chapter covers Spine-Leaf network topology and IP Fabric, with special attention paid to the abstraction of underlays and overlays. VXLAN is analyzed as a data plane protocol, BGP is analyzed as an underlay protocol, and BGP EVPN is analyzed as an overlay protocol.

\section{The Data Center}
\subsection{Evolution, Definition and Role}
The DC idea emerged due to centralization of the computing environment with computers kept in a room called a mainframe, thus being a single machine used by different terminals \cite{IBM_mainframe}. As per the client/server architecture that emerged in the 1980s, there were dedicated servers for file, printing, and database services, while clients handled interaction with users and contributed to larger data centers and the necessity to implement structured cabling \cite{IEEE_Evolution_of_Client_Server}. Internet-related services rendered data centers crucial for operations and necessitated high availability in the 1990s \cite{CERN_Data_Centre_Historical_Documentation}. The trend toward server virtualization such as VMware ESX in 2001 increased hardware efficiency but created networking problems due to the need to connect numerous virtual machines \cite{VMware_ESX}. Cloud computing popularized by providers like AWS, Azure, and GCP facilitated metered usage of scalable cloud infrastructures comprising up to hundreds of thousands of servers \cite{AWS_History_of_CC}. Nowadays, an emerging technology is artificial intelligence that prompts development of programmable data center infrastructures for large-scale applications that require parallel computing and low latency interconnects \cite{NVIDIA_AI_Inference_Whitepaper}.

\end{figure}

DC has been formally defined as a special facility that centralizes the information technology infrastructure of an organization in a highly available and controlled environment\cite{Uptime_Institute_Tier_Certification}. Beyond the hosting of hardware devices, the contemporary data center has become the essential operational platform for the information age, supporting every service, transaction, and communication in the interconnected world.

All DCs, regardless of their tier level, are constructed around three basic pillars, which interact with each other and define the overall performance of the DC\cite{Cisco_DC_design}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Compute: The computation layer consisting of servers, CPUs, and GPUs to perform tasks. Almost always virtualized, it breaks down physical systems into multiple independent virtual environments.
    \item Storage: The memory layer where data is stored beyond compute iterations. From direct-attached to fabric object storage, it determines data input/output speed and quality of service.
    \item Networking: The connectivity layer that connects computing and storage together. Being the most structured of the three pillars, it determines how fast data can be accessed and how reliably machines communicate.
\end{itemize}

\section{From Three-Tier to IP Fabric}
The architectures used to network these DCs have also undergone a revolutionary change over the past two decades or so. What started as a simple hierarchical model has now evolved into the programmable and distributed fabric model due to the influence of software-defined networking and virtualization of network services and hyperscale DCs.

\subsection*{The Three-Tier Model and Its Limits}
For several decades, the three-tier architecture was the de facto standard for network architecture in the DC and enterprises alike\cite{Campus_Network_Design_Guide}. The three-tier architecture was based on a three-tier model consisting of Access, Distribution, and Core, which provided a structured approach for interconnecting devices to the network, with each tier having a well-defined role in the forwarding model. Figure~\ref{fig:three-tier-model} depicts this traditional hierarchy.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \Architecturethreetier
    \caption{Three-Tier Architecture Model}
    \label{fig:three-tier-model}
\end{figure}

The Access layer is the essential connection of end devices such as servers, workstations, and storage devices to the network fabric. The Distribution layer is responsible of aggregating Access layer traffic and routing policy implementation. Lastly, the Core layer is in charge of high-speed connectivity between Distribution blocks and also deals with site or internet-bound traffic.
The three-tier network model is highly popular and widely applied, although it has some structural limitations, especially as the needs of the DCs increase. The pain points of the three-tier network model include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item STP Dependency: Spanning Tree Protocol is designed to block redundant links to prevent loops. This wastes active bandwidth and causes inefficiencies throughout the entire Access and Distribution hierarchy.
    \item Oversubscription: The aggregating nature of the network flows upward through the hierarchy creates potential bottlenecks in the Distribution and Core layers.
    \item East-West Traffic Problem: The original design of the network model is to handle north-south flows of clients and servers. However, the virtualized world creates server-to-server flows, and the inefficiencies in the forwarding tables have exposed fundamental flaws.
    \item Scalability Ceiling: To increase the network, more hierarchy is added, which increases the complexity.
    \item Large Failure Domains: A failure in the Distribution or Core layer can impact the entire segment of the network.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{SDN: Birth and Core Concepts}
The limitation of the three-tier model has led the networking industry to adopt a completely new paradigm, which separated the intelligence of the network from its forwarding hardware. Software-Defined Networking is a new paradigm in the field of networking, which originated as a research project in 2008 by Martin Casado and Scott Shenker of Stanford University and introduced the concept of OpenFlow as a protocol for the centralized control of network forwarding behavior.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \SDNarchitecture
    \caption{Software-Defined Networking Architecture}
    \label{fig:sdn-architecture}
\end{figure}
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:sdn-architecture}, the SDN architecture consists of three distinct layers.

In the Software-Defined Networking (SDN) architecture, there are three fundamental layers that are defined by a programmable interface between them. The top layer is the Application Layer where the application sends its intentions to the controller through the Northbound REST APIs. The middle layer is the Control Layer, which comprises the SDN controller, offering a global view of the whole network topology. The bottom layer is the Infrastructure Layer, which is made up of physical forwarding devices that are controlled by the SDN controller using the Southbound APIs, such as OpenFlow.

\subsection*{Network Functions Virtualization}
Coexisting alongside SDN, NFV was first formally defined by a group of major telecommunications operators through a white paper submitted to the ETSI standards body in 2012\cite{SDN_Architecture}. While SDN was addressing the programmability of the data plane, NFV was addressing a different issue related to the dependency of network functions on proprietary and dedicated hardware.
NFV was based on the idea of running firewalls, load balancers, intrusion detection systems, and WAN optimizers not on proprietary and dedicated hardware but on standard commercial-off-the-shelf servers. In other words, NFV was based on the idea of running network functions as software on top of servers. This would enable network functions to be deployed, scaled, and moved around dynamically, similar to how virtual machines are deployed, scaled, and moved around on top of physical servers. NFV and SDN are not competing technologies but are complementary to each other.

\subsection{The Emergence of the IP Fabric}
However, the early versions of OpenFlow architectures had the problems of single points of failure and scalability in terms of operating thousands of devices. In order to overcome this problem, proprietary approaches and IP Fabrics relied on a hybrid model, combining the centralized control plane with the resilience of routing protocol. Using the Flat Spine-Leaf architecture, IP Fabric leverages BGP for distributed control and VXLAN for virtualization of the data plane. The IP Fabric provides SDN-style programmability and scalability without having a single point of failure.

\section{Network Layering and Operational Planes}
\subsection*{Layering Models}
The OSI model, as proposed by ISO in 1984, divides the communication process into seven abstraction layers\cite{ISO_IEC_7498_1_1994}. Though it remains the normative reference for understanding the behavior of communication protocols and troubleshooting, it is not commonly implemented. The TCP/IP model, based on the ARPANET development and IETF standards, proposes a four-layer model that is actually implemented by real protocols\cite{IETFRFC1122}. Figure~\ref{fig:osi-vs-tcpip} compares the two models.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \TCPvsOSI
    \caption{OSI vs TCP/IP Layering Models}
    \label{fig:osi-vs-tcpip}
\end{figure}

In DC engineering, the TCP/IP model is the reference for operations, with every protocol used in the DC fabric mapped to one of four layers\cite{Data_Center_TCP/IP_Networking_Guide}. The OSI model is used as an analytical tool, particularly when thinking about where encapsulation boundaries occur or how an overlay protocol interacts with the underlying physical network.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data Plane}: This is the plane where the actual movement of packets happens. This is where the network operates, typically at the hardware level, through ASICs that make forwarding decisions without any involvement of software\cite{ASIC_Based_Forwarding_Architecture}. In this project, VXLAN is used as a data plane, where it encapsulates the original Layer 2 packets into a UDP packet, allowing it to traverse any IP network.
    \item \textbf{Control Plane}: This is the plane at which the network topologies are built and maintained. This is where routing protocols operate, where optimal paths are determined, and where network reachability information is disseminated to all the network elements that participated in the network \cite{IETFRFC4271}. In this project, BGP EVPN was used as a control plane where network MAC and IP information were disseminated to all VTEP elements in the network without using flood and learn.
    \item \textbf{Management Plane}: This layer is situated at a higher level than the other two and is the point of interface between the network and the network operator. This is where network operators configure, monitor, and automate the network \cite{NDFC_Product_Documentation}. The Cisco NDFC is situated at this plane in this project.
\end{itemize}
This division of the three planes is not merely theoretical but is the fundamental principle that allows DC networks to be at the same time scalable, programmable, and fault-tolerant. The data plane and the in-flight data do not get interrupted in the event of a failure in the control plane, and similarly, the management plane operations do not impact the data plane or the routing operations.

\section{Spine-Leaf and IP Fabric}
The Spine-Leaf topology is the fundamental basis upon which IP Fabrics are designed. The adoption of the Spine-Leaf topology in DC networking signifies a significant shift away from the traditional hierarchical structure of the three-tier topology, with its emphasis on vertical aggregation, to a flat, symmetric, and fully meshed topology, which is optimized for performance at scale.

\subsection*{Origins, Roles and Design Principles}
The Spine-Leaf topology is based on a variant of a Clos topology, a multistage switching topology proposed by Charles Clos at Bell Labs in 1952 to optimize telephone circuit switching via a non-blocking interconnection topology\cite{A_Study_of_Non-Blocking_Switching_Networks}. Re-discovered by hyperscalers such as Google and Facebook in the late 2000s as a solution to the exploding east-west traffic problem created by distributed computing workloads, the Clos topology has evolved into the two-tier Spine-Leaf topology now ubiquitous in DC networks\cite{Introducing_Data_Center_Fabric}. Figure~\ref{fig:spine-leaf} shows the typical Spine-Leaf layout.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \SpineLeafDiagram
    \caption{Spine-Leaf Topology}
    \label{fig:spine-leaf}
\end{figure}

The architecture is divided into two tiers with distinct responsibilities\cite{Cisco_Massively_Scalable_Data_Center_Network_Fabric_Design_and_Operation_White_Paper}. The first tier consists of spine nodes, which form the backbones of the fabric. They are responsible for routing between Leaf nodes. They are exclusively responsible for routing. They never connect to an end device. The second tier consists of Leaf nodes. They are access points for the fabric. A server, storage device, firewall, or gateway is only connected to a Leaf node. Importantly, no direct connections are allowed between Leaf nodes or between Spine nodes. Any two nodes can only communicate through exactly two hops: the source Leaf, the destination Leaf, and one spine node in between\cite{Cisco_Massively_Scalable_Data_Center_Network_Fabric_Design_and_Operation_White_Paper}. This ensures that latency is uniform throughout the fabric, irrespective of the communicating devices.

\subsection*{Oversubscription and Non-Blocking Ratios}
Oversubscription is defined as the ratio of device bandwidth to uplink capacity\cite{Oversubscription_in_Networking}. In traditional three-tier networks, this oversubscription occurs in Access, Distribution, and Core layers.

The Spine-Leaf network design avoids this issue by allocating Leaf to Spine bandwidth in direct proportion to server access capacity\cite{Non_Blocking_Data_Center_Fabric_Design}. This provides a non-blocking network where uplink capacity matches access demand, so application performance is not constrained by network architecture but rather by actual application demand.

\subsection*{Equal-Cost Multi-Path}
ECMP helps the full mesh Spine-Leaf topology function by using all paths at once\cite{IETFRFC2992}. This is different from the Spanning Tree Protocol, where it de-activates all paths to avoid loops in the network\cite{Understand_and_Manage_ECMP_Scale_in_Cisco_ACI_White_Paper}.

Since all paths between the Leafs and the Spine are now active paths, ECMP hashing of the flow based on flow identifiers such as IP and ports allows automatic load balancing and flow consistency\cite{Hashing_Algorithms_for_LAG_and_ECMP}. This allows the network to scale linearly with the number of paths provided.

\subsection{IP Fabric and Pod Architecture}
An IP Fabric is the logical structure resulting from combining a physical Spine-Leaf topology with a pure Layer 3 infrastructure, where all inter-switch paths are routed point-to-point interfaces and Layer 2 domains are confined strictly to server-facing Leaf ports\cite{IP_Fabric_for_Media_Design_Guide}. This entirely eliminates broadcast domains from the fabric core, replacing legacy instability and scalability issues with resilient routing protocols like BGP.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\overlayunderlay
\caption{ IP Fabric Underlay Concept}
\label{fig:ip-fabric-underlay}
\end{figure}
The underlay concept is visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:ip-fabric-underlay}.

To scale beyond a single Spine-Leaf set, the fabric uses a "Pod" as a modular, self-contained forwarding unit\cite{Facebook_Data_Center_Network_Architecture}. Multiple Pods interconnect via a Super Spine layer, allowing horizontal expansion rather than scaling individual switch capacity. This structure directly aligns with the multi-site intent of this project, where each data center site acts as an independent Pod connected over a Data Center Interconnect (DCI) layer to extend the IP Fabric across physical boundaries\cite{IETFRFC8365}.

\section{Underlay and Overlay}
The IP Fabric architecture is divided into two layers: underlay and overlay. The relationship between these two layers is essential in understanding how VXLAN and BGP EVPN operate in tandem as an integral part of the solution proposed in this project.

\subsection{The Underlay/The Overlay Network}
The underlay network is defined as the physical IP infrastructure that provides basic IP reachability between all nodes in the IP fabric network\cite{VXLAN_Network_with_MP-BGP_EVPN_Control_Plane_Design_Guide}. This network includes Spine and Leaf switches connected via routed point-to-point links .running a routing protocol to exchange IP reachability information among all VTEPs. There is no knowledge in this network of tenants, virtual machines, or logical network segments; its sole aim is to ensure that any node in the network is reachable via a routed IP path from any other node in the network.
The overlay network is a logical network running on top of the underlay network. It provides virtual network connectivity between network endpoints irrespective of their physical position within the network fabric\cite{IETFRFC7365}. It carries traffic within a tunnel, where the traffic is sent through the underlay network as normal IP packets. The underlay network is completely unaware of the inner packets. In this project, the VXLAN protocol is used for the encapsulation process, and the control plane is provided by the BGP EVPN.

\subsection{Why the Separation Matters}
The decoupling of the overlay from the underlay is the architectural choice that solves the fundamental issues identified in Chapter 1, as it allows the logical network topology to grow and expand independently of the physical topology beneath it\cite{IETFRFC8365}. A virtual machine move is possible, a new tenant segment is created, or a physical link fails, and none of these requires changes to the other topology. The underlay is the steady and constant IP transport, and the overlay is where all the complexity is handled.
BGP exchanges reachability information using path attributes, and can be hardened with mechanisms such as GTSM for directly connected peers\cite{IETFRFC4271,IETFRFC5082,Cisco_Nexus_9000_BGP_Configuration_Guide}.

\section{VXLAN - Virtual Extensible LAN}
\subsection{Context, Motivation and History}
The growth of virtualized servers and cloud computing resulted in two major problems for traditional networks: the limitations of segmentation and lack of flexibility. The IEEE 802.1Q VLAN protocol has a restriction in its design, limited to 12 bits, giving a total of only 4096 IDs. This is not enough for modern cloud providers working with multiple customers.

Also, Layer 2 DCs used STP, designed to avoid network loops, leaving most of their capacity unused. This made VMs dependent on their location since they had to maintain Layer 2 adjacency even while being moved around.

This problem was addressed by VXLAN RFC 7348\cite{IET_RFC_7348}, originated by VMware and Cisco. In contrast to the previous solutions, VXLAN uses Layer 3 underlay and encapsulates Ethernet frames into UDP/IP datagrams. Also, VXLAN increased the number of IDs to 24 bits, thus giving over 16 million segments.

\subsection{Encapsulation, VNI and Packet Format}
The VXLAN tunneling protocol functions based on the concept of MAC-in-UDP encapsulation\cite{IET_RFC_7348}. In this process, whenever an Ethernet frame is produced by a virtual machine, the VTEP at the access point captures this frame and encloses it within a series of headers that enable the routing of the frame over the underlying IP infrastructure. The actual frame remains intact, and the physical network perceives only the IP headers without knowing anything about the payload inside.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \VXLANDiagram
    \caption{VXLAN Encapsulation Diagram}
    \label{fig:vxlan-encapsulation-diagram}
\end{figure}
The encapsulation header format is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vxlan-encapsulation-diagram}. The encapsulated packet is structured from outermost to innermost as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Outer Ethernet Header: Contains the MAC addresses of neighboring physical nodes in the hop-by-hop forwarding path. This field changes with every Layer 3 hop in the underlay, just like in a typical routed IP packet.
    \item Outer IP Header: Contains the source and destination IP addresses for the two VTEPs involved in the tunnel. This information enables the encapsulated frame to pass through any IP network, irrespective of the geographical distance between the sites.
    \item Outer UDP Header: Utilizes the destination port number 4789, which was allocated by IANA specifically for VXLAN. Source port is calculated using the inner frame header hash, which enables distribution of traffic in an ECMP-based underlay.
    \item VXLAN Header: Holds the VNI, a 24-bit value that identifies which segment the inner frame is associated with and ensures that there can be 16 million segments available in isolation. The flags field is coded as “VVVV R000,” where the mandatory I flag should have a value of 1.
    \item Inner Ethernet Frame: Represents the actual Ethernet frame created by the originator VM. This inner frame remains untouched during transit and is invisible to the underlying physical layer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The VTEP}
The VXLAN Tunnel Endpoint (VTEP) is the network component tasked with encapsulation and decapsulation of all VXLAN packets, acting as the point where the physical underlay and virtual overlay connect. This acts as a bridge between regular Ethernet communications originating from local end-devices and remote VTEPs through the IP underlay using unique underlay IP addresses. VTEPs may exist as software components operating in the virtual switch of a hypervisor on commodity hardware or as hardware components operating in the Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) of a physical switch. In this case, hardware VTEPs are implemented in Cisco Nexus 9000 Leaf switches.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \VTEPtraffic
    \caption{VTEP Encapsulation and Decapsulation Process}
    \label{fig:vtep-diagram}
\end{figure}
The VTEP operation is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:vtep-diagram}.

In order to establish such connections, each of the VTEPs holds a forwarding table, which associates inner layer two MAC addresses with outer layer three IP addresses of the VTEPs where they exist. Upon receiving the packet from an external interface, the VTEP strips the outer header and finds out the particular Virtual Network Identifier (VNI). As a result, the original Ethernet frame is forwarded to the corresponding local network. Given that the mapping determines the way in which the traffic is directed, the scalability and effectiveness of the entire overlay network greatly depend on whether the forwarding table is built through inefficient data-plane flooding or an actual control plane protocol.

\section{BGP - Border Gateway Protocol}
\subsection{History, Evolution and DC Adoption}
BGP was originally created to solve inter-domain routing limitations during the explosive Internet growth of the late 1980s, when existing enterprise algorithms failed due to the requirement of maintaining full topological awareness. To address this, BGP-1 was introduced in 1989 via RFC 1105, establishing a path-vector approach that evolved through subsequent versions into the modern BGP-4 standard defined in RFC 4271\cite{IETFRFC1105,IETFRFC4271}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\BGPhistory
\caption{History of BGP Development}
\label{fig:bgp-history}
\end{figure}
Figure~\ref{fig:bgp-history} outlines the historical milestones of BGP.

While historically restricted to Autonomous System borders, BGP shifted inward to data centers because conventional IGPs like OSPF and IS-IS suffer from slow convergence and massive link-state database overhead in hyperscale topologies. Formalized by RFC 7938, using BGP within the fabric leverages its internet-scale path-vector scalability and policy control to completely bypass IGP limitations\cite{Cisco_IGP_Scalability_Limitations_DC_Fabrics,IETFRFC7938}. In modern layouts, BGP serves as the underlay routing protocol—deployed either as eBGP with unique ASNs per tier or as iBGP sharing a single ASN—ensuring a highly scalable and consistent protocol stack.

\subsection{iBGP vs eBGP}
BGP behavior differs significantly between eBGP and iBGP, which directly impacts fabric design. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:eBGPvsiBGP}, eBGP prepends the local AS to the AS\_PATH and sets TTL=1, requiring directly connected peers. iBGP uses the same AS, applies split‑horizon (requiring either a full mesh or Route Reflectors), and preserves the NEXT\_HOP attribute, which must be reachable via the underlying IGP. In a Spine‑Leaf IP Fabric, an eBGP design assigns unique AS numbers to each switch, while an iBGP design uses a single AS with Route Reflectors on the Spines to scale.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \ebgpibgp
    \caption{ the eBGP vs iBGP behavior in a Spine‑Leaf IP Fabric}
    \label{fig:eBGPvsiBGP}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Route Reflectors}
The requirement for a full iBGP mesh is architecturally impractical at scale, as $N$ nodes require $\frac{N(N-1)}{2}$ sessions; for instance, 500 peers would demand over 124,000 connections. RFC 4456 introduces Route Reflectors (RRs) to bypass split-horizon restrictions and reflect iBGP routes, eliminating the need for client-to-client sessions while non-clients maintain a full mesh\cite{IETFRFC4456}. An RR reflects paths using strict guidelines: routes from a client go to all peers, while routes from a non-client go only to clients. To prevent routing loops, RRs append an ORIGINATOR-ID (discarded if it matches the local Router ID) and a CLUSTER-LIST (tracking the cluster path like an AS-PATH)\cite{IETFRFC4456}.
\subsection*{BFD for Fast BGP Convergence}
Standard BGP fault detection relies on the Hold timer, which takes several seconds to recognize a peer failure and causes significant traffic blackholing\cite{IETFRF5880}. RFC 5880 defines Bidirectional Forwarding Detection (BFD) as a lightweight, protocol-independent mechanism that exchanges low-overhead control packets to reduce detection times to milliseconds\cite{BFDConfiguration}.

In this architecture, BFD acts alongside eBGP between Leaf and Spine switches to accelerate fault recovery.

\section{BGP - EVPN}
\subsection{Context, History and Definition}
Whereas VXLAN successfully addressed Layer 2 scale and geographically bounded limitations, it brought about its own set of problems. In terms of the flood-and-learn paradigm, regardless of whether multicast or head-end replication was used, endpoint discovery became a task of the data plane: all unknown destinations were flooded until the forwarding table was dynamically built through reactive learning\cite{IETFRFC7365}.

A solution to this problem has been proposed in a completely unrelated field. Initially described in RFC 7432 (2015), EVPN is a control-plane mechanism for MPLS-based L2VPNs. EVPN replaced the traditional VLAN service VPLS and enabled distribution of MAC reachability via BGP rather than data-plane flooding \cite{IETFRFC7365}. In EVPN, both MAC and IP reachability information are contained in a single BGP address family. Its extension to VXLAN was detailed in RFC 8365 (2018).

EVPN can be regarded as an additional BGP address family (AFI 25 / SAFI 70) operating in ordinary MP-BGP sessions. There are various route types, each carrying a particular class of overlay reachability information. On discovering a new endpoint, the originating VTEP builds an appropriate EVPN route and distributes it to other BGP speakers. Upon receipt of such routes, remote VTEPs build forwarding entries proactively.

\subsection*{From VPLS to EVPN}
The BGP EVPN protocol is a development of VPLS, which was the leading Layer 2 virtual private network technology. The VPLS network architecture was a mesh of pseudowires with data-plane-based learning, whereby every PE would learn all the MAC addresses in an inefficient manner. Broadcasting increased proportionally with the square of the number of PEs, multihoming support was unstandardized and proprietary, and there was no mechanism in VPLS to distribute IP reachability information\cite{IETFRFC8365}.

BGP EVPN addressed all these issues through the adoption of proactive MAC learning based on BGP, thus utilizing its scaling and policy mechanisms. Ethernet Segment was developed as a standardized solution for multihoming, and a single address family for MAC/IP binding was introduced, which made flooding obsolete and allowed for concurrent routing and bridging.

\subsection{EVPN Route Types}
There are five different route types defined by BGP EVPN, where each route type is a representation of different information about overlay reachability encapsulated in BGP NLRI\cite{IETFRFC7365}. In total, all the route types are required for VTEP to properly switch traffic within the fabric network based on different pieces of information, such as endpoint identity, segmentation, BUM traffic, multihoming, and inter-VRF routing. All the route types are advertised using BGP UPDATE messages.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Type 1 - Ethernet Auto-Discovery Route:}
Used in multihomed environments to facilitate fast convergence and split-horizon filtering \cite{IETFRFC7432}. It enables "mass withdrawal," allowing a VTEP to revoke reachability for all MAC addresses on an Ethernet Segment with a single update, and prevents traffic loops in active-active deployments.

\subsection{How EVPN Drives VXLAN}
BGP EVPN and VXLAN form a complementary control-to-data plane pipeline where EVPN pre-populates the exact reachability information that VXLAN requires for data forwarding, resolving traditional flood-and-learn limitations as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:EvpnDrivesVXLan}. When a virtual machine connects to a leaf, the local VTEP avoids flooding by immediately advertising a Type 2 MAC/IP route to the Spine Route Reflectors, which distribute it to remote leaves to build precise forwarding tables without ARP or data-plane flooding. Similarly, broadcast, unknown unicast, and multicast (BUM) traffic is tightly controlled via Type 3 Inclusive Multicast Routes, mapping underlay IPs into a head-end replication list that allows the control plane to deliver efficient, predictable unicast VXLAN tunneling across the entire fabric.

\subsection*{ARP Suppression}
By leveraging the proactive exchange of Type 2 MAC/IP bindings, BGP EVPN introduces ARP suppression to eliminate the costly broadcast storms and fabric-wide BUM flooding typical of unmanaged VXLAN networks. Local Leaf switches intercept incoming ARP queries directly and respond as a proxy using their fully synchronized EVPN tables, ensuring that the broadcast domain stays confined within a single leaf. This suppression model achieves zero fabric-wide BUM traffic for address resolution, saving valuable underlay bandwidth and CPU cycles across remote endpoints.

\subsection{*MAC Mobility}
To prevent traffic from blackholing during virtual machine live migrations across distributed switches, BGP EVPN utilizes an explicit sequence-numbering mechanism embedded within Type 2 advertisements to track host movement dynamically, as demonstrated in Figure~\ref{fig:MACmobility}. When a VM shifts to a new Leaf, the receiving VTEP increments the sequence number in its Type 2 route community, prompting all remote VTEPs to immediately update their paths to the higher value and forcing the old Leaf to withdraw its stale entry.

\subsection*{L2VNI and L3VNI}

\subsection*{Symmetric vs Asymmetric IRB}
Integrated Routing and Bridging (IRB) allows Leaf nodes to execute simultaneous Layer 2 bridging and Layer 3 routing, with Symmetric IRB offering far superior $O(N)$ state scalability over the resource-heavy Asymmetric model, as analyzed in Table~\ref{tab:irb_comparison}. \subsection*{Anycast Gateway}
To eliminate the trombone routing inefficiencies of traditional FHRP architectures where inter-subnet traffic must loop through a distant active router, EVPN-VXLAN implements a distributed anycast gateway directly inside every Leaf node, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:AnycastGateway}. By assigning an identical virtual IP and MAC address across all participating leaves using Cisco NDFC, any local Leaf switch can instantly intercept and route cross-subnet traffic at the source. This architecture integrates seamlessly with ARP suppression because the default gateway MAC remains well-known to all nodes, meaning virtual machines route traffic locally without executing fabric-wide broadcast discovery queries.

\subsection*{Multi-Tenancy and VRF Design}
Multi-tenancy isolates tenant traffic on shared hardware by mapping individual VRFs to unique L3VNI transit tunnels, separating routing, ARP, and forwarding tables. To maintain prefix uniqueness across tenants with overlapping IP schemes, BGP EVPN appends a 64-bit Route Distinguisher (RD) to fabric routes, while Route Target (RT) extended communities strictly control prefix imports and exports. This framework creates a secure multi-tenant boundary where duplicate addresses coexist seamlessly, guaranteeing absolute isolation unless cross-VRF route leaking is explicitly configured via RT policies\cite{IETFRFC8365,VXLAN_Network_with_MP-BGP_EVPN_Control_Plane_Design_Guide}. Each tenant operates within its own closed routing space, entirely unaware of other entities sharing the underlying physical switches. Ultimately, this logical division allows administrators to scale the infrastructure securely while maintaining strict compliance and administrative boundaries for every distinct organization.
